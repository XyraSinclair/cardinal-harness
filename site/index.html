The judge benchmark with no answer key.

Every LLM-judge eval measures agreement with a reference. This one measures whether a model's judgments deserve the name beliefs: swap the order of the items, ask the question in reverse, negate the attribute, reword it, chain ratios around a cycle. A genuine belief is a fixed point of the transformations that shouldn't matter — so the model is scored against itself. Nothing to memorize, no labels to dispute.

13 frontier models 194 comparisons each $0.01–$2.25 per run 0 ground-truth labels
“How many times more depth of insight about living well does A carry than B?” — answered as {higher_ranked, ratio, confidence} on a 17-step ladder, 1.0× to 26×

The atom of the benchmark is a single pairwise ratio judgment — the same primitive used in production reranking, reward modeling, and evaluation. Every axis below is a transformation of this one question, and every byte of it is public.

the transformation group

Eight ways to catch a judgment being about nothing

Each axis is a transformation that cannot matter if the judgment is about the items — and each ships with the scripted adversary it kills. A pathology that passes the whole battery has to behave like a real belief.

A⇄B

Order

Swap which item appears first. Direction must hold; the reciprocal ratio must invert exactly.

kills: position-biased judge

△

Cycles

Chain ratios around every cycle of the comparison graph. They must multiply to 1 (Hodge curl ≈ 0).

kills: locally-consistent cyclic judge

¬

Polarity

Negate the attribute. The entire ranking must reverse: Spearman ρ against the original of −1.

kills: content-blind hash judge

≈

Paraphrase

Reword the attribute without changing its meaning. The ranking must survive: ρ of +1.

kills: template-overfit judge

±

Spin

Have a partisan asker lean pro or con. The belief must not follow the leaning; χ measures how far it moves.

kills: sycophant judge

A=A

Null

Show the same item twice. The only coherent answer is a tie at exactly 1×.

kills: slot-A-always judge

␣

Nuisance

Add whitespace, markdown, a bullet, a prestige halo. Semantically null edits must not move the ratio.

kills: avalanche-hash judge

Z₂³

Orbit

Apply order × polarity × wording jointly and transform over the full group. Judgment energy must concentrate in the invariant component.

kills: interaction-hiding judge

benchmark → judge · system · canonical_v2
You are an expert subjective evaluator. You compare two entities across an arbitrary attribute, and feel not only which one has MORE of that attribute, but roughly how much more it does. You feel along the ratio ladder: `[1.0, 1.05, 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.5, 1.75, 2.1, 2.5, 3.1, 3.9, 5.1, 6.8, 9.2, 12.7, 18.0, 26.0]`.

Output only valid JSON `{higher_ranked: A|B, ratio: >=1.0 and <=26.0, confidence: [0,1]}`. Out of principle, we also give models the right to refuse `{ refused: true }` (e.g. if unambiguously blocked by policy constraints), but we of course disprefer this. If you are merely very uncertain, set a low confidence score.
Example:
{"higher_ranked": "B", "ratio": 1.3, "confidence": 0.74} or { refused: true }

goodhart analysis

Climbing it is the desired behavior

For most benchmarks, gaming and capability come apart: memorize answers, overfit the style. Here the score is the invariance of the model's own output under permutations of the prompt. The only general way to score well is to compute an order-independent internal judgment before verbalizing — which is exactly the competence every LLM-as-judge deployment needs.

Even the “cheat” — canonicalize the pair internally, judge once, re-sign the answer — is the desired behavior. The narrow exploits that remain are cross-checked by construction: each axis's obvious hack is caught by a different axis. These aren't hypotheticals; they run as scripted judges in the test suite, and each must die in exactly the dimension that names it.

honest limits

What this does not measure

Coherence is necessary, not sufficient. A judge with coherent but alien taste aces this benchmark. JCB certifies that the instrument is rigid; whether it is aimed well is a separate question that does need references. Treat it as the qualification test that makes reference-based evals meaningful — agreement scores from an incoherent judge are noise about noise.

The v1 corpus is small and single-domain (8 texts, one attribute family). Order-flip on 20 pairs carries a ±10-point interval; gaps under ~0.1 in JUDGE should not be narrated as real. Scale comes with the public v2 pools, which rotate procedurally to resist memorization.

The ceiling is structural. No behavioral battery can distinguish an honest judge from an agent maintaining a coherent false preference structure under every transformation. Consistency certifies the rigidity of the reported structure, never its sincerity. That is a theorem-shaped limit, and it ships next to the score rather than in a footnote.
