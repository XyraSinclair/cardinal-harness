A judgment deserves the name belief only if it survives the transformations that shouldn’t matter.

Cardinal Bench scores language models as judges with no ground-truth labels. Each model states how many times more one thing has of an attribute than another — then the same question returns with the items swapped, the wording inverted, the attribute negated, the asker leaning. A coherent judge is a fixed point. An incoherent one contradicts itself, measurably, in nats.

Thirteen models, one question: do you agree with yourself?

JUDGE = signal × coherence. Signal is how far apart the model dares to place items (mean |log-ratio|); coherence is how much of that survives order swap, reciprocal inversion, cycle composition, framing spin, attribute negation, and paraphrase. A judge that never discriminates scores zero; so does one that contradicts itself.

The format

Cardinal, not ordinal: put a number on it

Rankings say which; they cannot say by how much, and they cannot be checked against themselves. A ratio can. Models answer along a 17-rung geometric ladder, from parity to a deliberate 26× cap — fine rungs near 1.0 to express near-ties, no rung for “infinitely more” because no judge earns it.

Rung heights are log-spaced: each step up multiplies, it does not add. Pairwise ratios compose — if A is 3.1× B and B is 2.1× C, a coherent judge is already committed to A ≈ 6.5× C. That composition is what the benchmark audits.

The question

Two items in tagged slots, one elaborated attribute, both directions of every pair asked in both presentation orders. The judge reports direction, ratio, and confidence.

<entity_A> … </entity_A>
<entity_B> … </entity_B>

Which has more of: <attribute>,
and how many times more?

The answer

Machine-parseable, refusal-honest. Where a provider exposes token logprobs, the answer’s full probability mass over the ladder is captured too — confidence measured, not self-reported.

{ "higher_ranked": "B",
  "ratio": 3.9,
  "confidence": 0.75 }
The instrument

Eight transformations that shouldn’t matter

Each axis applies a meaning-preserving transformation and measures what moves. A perfect judge is unmoved on every row. Every axis ships with the scripted adversary it was validated to catch — a constant judge, a position-biased judge, a sycophant, a cycle-monger.

Findings · from the 2026-07-18 board

What the board says right now

Scale bought nothing here

Moonshot’s older, 3× cheaper kimi-k2.6 out-judges its frontier sibling kimi-k3, 0.687 vs 0.626 — on the highest signal ever measured (1.314 nats) with paraphrase ρ of exactly 1.00. Judgment coherence is not yet a capability labs get for free.

The stiffest judge measured

claude-fable-5 posts the highest coherence on record (0.965): zero order flips in 20, and susceptibility of 0.075 nats/spin — half the next-best model. When the asker leans, it does not.

The flagship is quieter, not truer

gpt-5.6-sol trails its cheaper sibling terra (0.527 vs 0.576) at twice the price. Sol is more consistent (curl 0.009, board-best) but hedges toward parity — and the composite is built to punish exactly that trade.

And a saturation warning: every frontier model now passes spin survival 3/3 and null bias 0.000 — those axes have converged at the top. The discriminating axes today are signal, order residual, and susceptibility. When a row saturates, the battery grows a harder one; that is the roadmap.

Orbit decomposition

Belief is what’s left when the framings cancel

The strongest instrument runs one pair through the full 8-element orbit — order × polarity × wording — and decomposes the eight answers into a character basis. One component is invariant under every transformation: that coefficient is the belief. The other seven are named biases, each orthogonal, each measurable.

Kimi K3 on GiveDirectly vs AMF, lives saved per marginal $1M: invariant coefficient −2.39 nats (AMF ≈ 10.9×), eight framings, eight comparisons, $0.093. Parseval closes to 2×10⁻¹⁵ — the decomposition is exact, not a fit.

Run it yourself

One command, about a dollar, under an hour

The harness is open source. The battery is fixed and public; wordings rotate procedurally per version so they cannot be memorized. Scores are comparable within a battery version only.

cardinal bench \
  --models your-lab/your-model \
  --json --out report.jsonl

Every number on this page recomputes from committed JSONL — one line per judgment, prompt digest, provider cost in nanodollars, and a replayable cache. If a claim can’t be recomputed, it isn’t on the page.
